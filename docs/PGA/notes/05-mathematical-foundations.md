## 5.1 Historical Overview

Two 16th-century Frenchmen laid the groundwork:

- **Descartes** → analytic geometry, Cartesian coordinates → today's VLAAG (vector/linear algebra + analytic geometry)
- **Desargues** → projective geometry, with **ideal points** where parallel lines meet, and the principle of **duality** (point ↔ plane, join ↔ meet are interchangeable; truth is preserved)

In the 19th century, **Cayley & Klein** showed how to embed a euclidean metric into projective space using a **quadratic form** — the Cayley-Klein model. Meanwhile **Hamilton** (quaternions) and **Grassmann** (exterior algebra) invented new algebraic structures. **Clifford** (1878) unified all of this into **geometric algebra**.

## 5.2 Vector Spaces

A real vector space V of dimension n: closed under addition and scalar multiplication. Choosing a basis gives coordinates.

Every V has an isomorphic **dual space** V* of co-vectors. A co-vector θ is a linear functional: ⟨θ, v⟩ ∈ R.

When n=3, v can be interpreted as a **line through the origin**, θ as a **plane through the origin**, and ⟨θ, v⟩ = 0 means v lies in the plane.

## 5.3 Normed Vector Spaces

A vector space alone can't measure angles or distances. Introduce a **symmetric bilinear form** B: V × V → R satisfying:

1. B(αu₁ + βu₂, v) = αB(u₁, v) + βB(u₂, v) — bilinearity
2. B(u, v) = B(v, u) — symmetry

This becomes an inner product: **u·v := B(u, v)**, and defines a norm: **‖u‖ := √|u·u|**.

## 5.4 Sylvester Signature Theorem

Every symmetric bilinear form of dimension n is characterized by three non-negative integers (p, m, z) with p + m + z = n. There exists an orthogonal, normalized basis {eᵢ} such that:

```
eᵢ·eⱼ = 0  for i ≠ j

eᵢ·eᵢ =  1  for 1 ≤ i ≤ p       (positive)
         -1  for p < i ≤ p+m    (negative)
          0  for p+m < i ≤ n    (degenerate/zero)
```

**Example:** R³ with (3,0,0) gives the familiar euclidean vector space with ‖(x,y,z)‖ = √(x²+y²+z²).

## 5.5 Euclidean Space Eⁿ

**Key distinction:** Rⁿ (euclidean vector space) and Eⁿ (euclidean space) are different:

- Rⁿ contains **vectors**; motions are rotations preserving the origin
- Eⁿ contains **points**; motions include both rotations and translations

Eⁿ is built by identifying each vector of Rⁿ (including 0-vector = origin O) with a point, then defining distance d(P,Q) := ‖P − Q‖. This creates a differentiable manifold whose tangent space at every point is Rⁿ.

PGA unifies both Rⁿ and Eⁿ in "one organic whole."

## 5.6 The Tensor Algebra T(V)

The tensor algebra is generated by the **tensor product ⊗** (bilinear). The tensor product of k vectors is a **k-vector** forming the vector space Tₖ. T₀ = R. The full algebra is the direct sum:

```
T(V) = T₀ ⊕ T₁ ⊕ T₂ ⊕ T₃ ⊕ ...
```

This is a huge, unwieldy structure — but necessary as a stepping stone.

**Example (n=2):** With basis {u, v}:
- T₀: {1}
- T₁: {u, v}
- T₂: {u⊗u, u⊗v, v⊗u, v⊗v}
- T₃: {u⊗u⊗u, u⊗u⊗v, ...} (8 elements)
- etc.

## 5.7 Exterior Algebra ⋀(V)

Obtained from T(V) by declaring **v⊗v ∼ 0** — squares of 1-vectors vanish. By bilinearity:

```
(u+v)⊗(u+v) ∼ 0  ⇒  u⊗v ∼ −v⊗u
```

So the quotient product is **anti-symmetric** on 1-vectors. This is the **exterior (wedge) product**, written **X ∧ Y**. Properties:

- Anti-symmetric on 1-vectors
- Associative
- Distributive over addition
- X ∧ Y = 0 ⟺ X and Y are linearly dependent
- Otherwise X ∧ Y is the (k+m)-vector representing the **span** of the subspaces

Two k-vectors v and αv represent the **same subspace** but with different intensities (weights).

The exterior algebra is **finite-dimensional**: any m-vector with m > n vanishes (a product of n+1 basis 1-vectors has a repeated factor → 0). Total dimension = Σⁿᵢ₌₀ C(n,i) = 2ⁿ.

**Example (n=2):** With basis {u, v}:
- ⋀⁰: {1} — scalars
- ⋀¹: {u, v} — 1-vectors (lines)
- ⋀²: {u∧v} — 2-vectors (points)

## 5.8 The Dual Exterior Algebra ⋀*(V)

The dual space V* generates its own exterior algebra **⋀(V*) = ⋀*(V)**. Key difference:

| | Standard ⋀(V) | Dual ⋀*(V) |
|---|---|---|
| 1-vectors | lines through origin | hyperplanes through origin |
| Wedge operation | subspace **join** | subspace **meet** |

The two are completely equivalent by duality. Both are needed for PGA.

## 5.9 Projective Space RPⁿ⁻¹

Projectivize an n-dimensional V: identify vectors that are nonzero scalar multiples of each other:

```
u ∼ v  ⟺  ∃λ ≠ 0 such that u = λv
```

The points of RPⁿ⁻¹ are the lines through the origin of V.

**Example — RP²:** Start with R³ (basis {e₀, e₁, e₂}). Each point P on the z=1 plane represents a line through origin. Lines through the origin in the z=0 plane don't intersect z=1 — these become **ideal points**. The intersection of parallel planes yields an **ideal line**.

**Why projectivize?** The meet of parallel lines/planes and the join of euclidean and ideal elements are handled without special cases. Only in projective space can we represent translations.

## 5.10 Projective Exterior Algebras

Apply projectivization to the Grassmann algebras to get **P(⋀(V))** and **P(⋀*(V))**. Using (n+1)-dimensional V yields RPⁿ.

In **P(⋀)** — the standard algebra:
- Grade 1 = points, grade 2 = lines, grade 3 = planes, ...
- ∧ = projective **join**
- Grade-k elements represent subspaces of dimension k−1

In **P(⋀*)** — the dual algebra:
- Grade 1 = hyperplanes, grade n = points
- ∧ = projective **meet**

**Poincaré duality map J:** P(⋀) ↔ P(⋀*), maps x → x*. Grade-reversing: ⋀ᵏ ↔ ⋀ⁿ⁺¹⁻ᵏ. J² = id.

Using J, you can **import** operations between algebras. For example, define a join operator ∨ in P(⋀*) via:

```
X ∨ Y := J(J(X) ∧ J(Y))
```

where the right-hand ∧ is from P(⋀). This makes both **join (∨) and meet (∧)** available within a single algebra. The imported operation is called the **regressive product**.

> In this document: ∧ = meet (in P(⋀*)), ∨ = join (imported from P(⋀)). Think: ∧ ≈ ∩, ∨ ≈ ∪.
